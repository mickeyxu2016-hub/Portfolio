The Business Problem

Brand teams in heavy-industry categories often run on long-cycle research — annual brand-tracker studies, occasional pulse surveys. By the time a finding lands, the conversation has moved on. Social listening flips that: real-time customer voice, every day, scraped straight from open digital conversation.

The question was simple: in a single week of digital chatter, what can we learn about how Komatsu is positioned against its competitors — and where should the brand push next?

The core question: What does the market actually say about Komatsu — not what we hope it says?

The Three Lenses

The analysis tracked three complementary brand-health signals — each answers a different strategic question:

📣 Mention Volume
How loud is each brand in the conversation? Volume = attention. Sudden spikes signal a launch, a crisis, or a competitor moment to watch.
💚 Sentiment
Are mentions positive, neutral, or negative? Tracks the quality of attention — whether a brand is being celebrated or criticized.
🎯 Share of Voice
What slice of the category conversation does Komatsu own vs. each rival? The relative-position metric that turns raw volume into competitive insight.

How the Study Worked

01

Scope & Keyword Setup

Defined the competitive set, the tracking keywords, and the language/region filters — making sure the study captured the right conversations and filtered out unrelated noise (e.g. unrelated uses of brand names).

02

Mentionlytics Listening Run

Configured Mentionlytics to collect mentions across social media, news, blogs, and forums over the 7-day window — capturing every reference to Komatsu and each competitor.

03

Volume & Sentiment Tagging

Pulled the raw mentions and the AI-classified sentiment scores (positive / neutral / negative) for each brand, day-over-day across the study window.

04

Share-of-Voice Modeling

Aggregated mentions into share-of-voice percentages — what portion of the category conversation each brand owned — and benchmarked Komatsu's slice against the leader and the runner-ups.

05

Strategic Recommendations

Synthesized the three lenses into a one-page strategic brief: where Komatsu is winning, where it's losing ground, and which positioning moves the data supports.

What Made It Different

  • Three lenses, not one. Mention volume alone tells you who's being talked about; sentiment alone tells you how they're being talked about; share-of-voice alone tells you the relative split. The three together tell you which competitor is gaining the right kind of attention — the only insight that actually drives positioning decisions.
  • Days, not quarters. Traditional brand-tracker studies update on annual or quarterly cycles. A 7-day social listening study is fast, cheap, and repeatable — a different unit of analysis that complements (rather than replaces) the long-cycle work.
  • AI tagging, human framing. Mentionlytics handles classification at scale; the value-add is the strategic frame put around the numbers. A spike in negative mentions only matters if you can say why, and what to do about it.
  • One-page brief discipline. A week of data could become a hundred-slide deck. Forcing the synthesis to fit a one-page strategic brief made the recommendations decision-ready, not just thorough.

Impact

  • Delivered data-backed positioning recommendations identifying where Komatsu was winning, where it was losing ground, and where to push the brand narrative next
  • Established a repeatable social-listening workflow — same keyword set, same three lenses, runnable monthly — to turn ad-hoc competitive scans into an ongoing brand-health signal
  • Showed how AI-powered listening can complement traditional brand tracking — operating on a weekly cadence instead of a quarterly one, and at a fraction of the cost
  • Produced an executive-grade one-page brief that translated a week of raw mentions into a focused set of positioning moves

What I Learned

  • Volume is loud; sentiment is signal. The brands talked about most weren't always the brands talked about best. Pairing the two metrics caught nuances either one would have missed alone.
  • Competitive context changes the story. Komatsu's absolute volume looked different once it was reframed as share-of-voice — what looked like growth was actually losing ground to a competitor growing faster. Relative metrics often beat absolute ones.
  • Synthesis is the deliverable. Mentionlytics gives you charts. A useful study gives you a decision. The hardest, highest-value step was turning a week of dashboards into three things the brand should actually do.

The Business Problem

Theory built its name on tailored, business-focused apparel — sharp suiting, the clean office uniform. But the Chinese premium-apparel consumer was moving fast: younger shoppers were spending more on self-expression and lifestyle, not just professional wear, and the brand's existing positioning was leaving growth on the table.

The client wanted to evolve from "business wear" to "lifestyle luxury" — but a brand repositioning is only as good as the consumer evidence behind it. The question was: what does the Chinese premium consumer actually want, where is Theory underperforming today, and which strategic moves matter most?

The core question: Out of dozens of consumer signals, which few should Theory actually act on first?

Data Overview

I integrated consumer data from three categories to triangulate a single view of the premium-apparel consumer in China:

Internal Kantar Datasets
Proprietary survey responses on consumer attitudes, purchase drivers, brand perception, and product attribute importance vs. satisfaction.
CBNData
Chinese consumer-trend reports covering category-level spending shifts, generational behavior, and channel preference data.
199IT
Industry research aggregating market sizing, competitor performance, and macro retail trends across the Chinese fashion industry.

The Prioritization Framework

The hardest part of any consumer-insights project is the same: dozens of attributes, each with a survey score — which ones actually deserve client action?

I built an Excel-based prioritization framework that scored every strategic attribute on three dimensions, instead of the usual one or two. This is what made the recommendations defensible and focused rather than a long flat list:

µ
Mean Importance
How important is this attribute to consumers, on average? The baseline filter — separates "table stakes" from "doesn't matter."
σ
Standard Deviation
How much do consumers agree on the importance? Low SD = consensus the brand can confidently lean into. High SD = a segmented opportunity, not a universal one.
Δ
Unmet Importance
The gap between importance and satisfaction. The bigger the gap, the bigger the white-space opportunity for Theory to win on something its competitors haven't.

Combining the three dimensions sorted every attribute into one of four strategic buckets — priority innovation, innovation opportunity, maintain, or deprioritize — turning a 30-attribute table into a short, defensible action list the client could actually use.

What Made the Analysis Different

  • Three dimensions, not two. Classic importance × satisfaction analysis ranks attributes on two axes. Adding standard deviation as a third lens revealed which "high importance" findings were universal vs. driven by a vocal segment — and prevented the client from over-investing in polarizing attributes.
  • Multi-source triangulation. No single data source tells the truth alone. Cross-referencing Kantar's first-party consumer panel with CBNData's category-level trends and 199IT's market sizing meant every recommendation was anchored in at least two independent signals.
  • From analysis to action. The four-bucket output (priority innovate / innovate / maintain / deprioritize) mapped directly to client decision-making — what to build next, what to defend, what to stop spending against. Analysts deliver tables; this delivered a roadmap.
  • Built for storytelling. Every chart went through a visual-narrative pass — same numbers, but the question they answered was always visible at the top. The Canva-finished decks were the artifact the client actually used, not the raw notebooks.

Impact

  • Delivered data-backed recommendations shaping Theory's brand repositioning from business-focused to lifestyle/luxury in the Chinese premium-apparel market
  • Reframed a 30-attribute survey output into a focused action list the client could prioritize against, instead of a flat table of scores
  • Contributed insights on brand image refinement, CRM optimization, and competitive positioning that fed directly into consulting deliverables and executive presentations
  • Built a reusable prioritization framework the team could apply to other premium-apparel brand projects beyond the Theory engagement

What I Learned

  • The framework is the deliverable. Clients don't pay for a table of survey numbers — they pay for a defensible way to choose what matters. The three-dimensional scoring was the real product; the charts were just how it got communicated.
  • Variance is signal, not noise. Looking at SD alongside the mean changed which recommendations I was willing to put in front of the client. It's a habit that carried into every analytical project after this one.
  • Storytelling is technical. The same number told in two different ways drives two different decisions. Spending real time on Tableau and Canva visual choices wasn't decoration — it was the difference between a slide that lands and a slide that gets skipped.
